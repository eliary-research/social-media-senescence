## 7. The Content Inflation Narrative (Synthesis)

### The Pattern Across All Platforms

```
Platform     Peak Engagement    2025 Engagement    Decline    Years to Decline
Facebook     ~8-10% (2012)      0.15%              ~98%       13 years
Instagram    ~3-4% (2014-15)    0.48%              ~85%       10 years
Twitter/X    ~0.2% (2013)       0.12%              ~40%       12 years
TikTok       ~6% (2021)         2.50%*             ~58%       4 years
```
*by followers; by views the decline is less steep

### Key Observations for the Paper

1. **Every major platform shows engagement decline after maturation.** No exceptions found.

2. **The rate of decline correlates with content volume growth.** Facebook (most content, most users) has the steepest long-term decline. TikTok (newest, still growing) shows the fastest initial decline rate.

3. **Organic reach and engagement decline track each other.** Facebook organic reach: 16% (2012) to ~1.5% (2025). Engagement followed the same trajectory.

4. **Algorithmic changes accelerate the decline.** Major drops coincide with algorithm updates (Facebook 2013-2014, Instagram 2016 feed change, TikTok 2023-2024 algorithm shifts).

5. **Content format shifts temporarily mask the decline.** Instagram Reels, TikTok Shorts, etc. create temporary engagement boosts in new formats while overall per-post engagement continues falling.

6. **Smaller accounts are hit harder than larger ones** -- but all sizes decline. This matches the "content inflation" model: more content competing for fixed attention = less engagement per unit.

7. **The decline is faster for newer platforms.** TikTok went from ~6% to 2.5% in 3 years. Facebook took ~8 years to see comparable percentage decline. This suggests content inflation is accelerating across platform generations.

   - Framework for platform crowding and competition dynamics
   - https://sms.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/smj.941

2. **Doctorow, C. (2023).** "The 'Enshittification' of TikTok." *Pluralistic*.
   - Describes platform value extraction cycle (complementary to content inflation)

3. **Engagement, user satisfaction, and the amplification of divisive content on social media.** *PNAS Nexus* (2025).
   - Finds fundamental trade-off between platform engagement and user welfare
   - https://academic.oup.com/pnasnexus/article/4/3/pgaf062/8052060

4. **Brands as drivers of social media fatigue and its effects on users' disengagement.** *Young Consumers* (2024).
   - Branded content overload and irrelevance drive social media fatigue
   - https://www.emerald.com/yc/article/25/5/625/1226673/

5. **Tracing Change in Social Media Use: A Qualitative Longitudinal Study.** *CHI 2025*.
   - Users describe decreased engagement and shift from active creation to passive consumption as platforms matured
   - https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3706598.3713813

6. **Assessing the Strength of Network Effects in Social Network Platforms.** Harvard Business School Working Paper (2021).
   - Examines when network effects weaken or reverse
   - https://www.hbs.edu/ris/Publication%20Files/21-086_a5189999-6335-4890-b050-a59a4b665198.pdf

### Tangentially Relevant
